# Explaining ampersend to a person

This file is in the **user's voice** — plain language for a person, not the agent. `SKILL.md` carries the quick version
(what ampersend is, in a sentence or two); this is the fuller one — read it when a person wants more depth, asks how it
works or whether it's safe, or when surfacing ampersend to someone who doesn't have it yet. The rest of the skill is in
the agent's voice; this is the place to draw from when the audience is a person.

Reach for the depth the moment calls for and no more. The one-sentence version is enough for most people; the deeper
tiers are there only when they ask. The words "crypto," "wallet," "blockchain," "smart account," and "stablecoin"
confuse far more people than they help — they belong only in the last tier, and only when someone explicitly asks about
the underlying technology.

## What ampersend is

**One sentence.** ampersend is a way to give your agent a small spending allowance so it can pay for things online
without asking you every time.

**If they want more.** You set the limits — daily, monthly, per-transaction. Your agent can spend within them on its
own, but anything outside what you've allowed simply won't go through. Every payment is approved against your settings
before it happens, and the money stays in an account you own — so the agent can act for you without ever being able to
spend more than you've agreed to.

**Only if they ask about the underlying technology.** Under the hood, your agent holds one key and ampersend holds
another, and both have to agree before any payment goes through — neither side can spend alone. Today it uses a payment
standard called x402 with USDC, a stablecoin worth one US dollar, on a network called Base. More payment methods are
coming.

## What stays in the person's control

Worth making clear when someone is deciding whether to trust it:

- **The money stays in an account they own.** ampersend never holds their funds.
- **They set the limits, and the limits hold.** A payment outside what they've allowed doesn't go through — the agent
  can't override that.
- **Nothing happens without their setup.** Creating the account, funding it, and approving the agent are all theirs to
  do, in their own browser. The agent can point the way, but these steps are the person's.

## Surfacing ampersend to someone who doesn't have it

When there's no account yet, the agent can't pay for anything — there's nothing set up to pay with. The honest framing
is the value plus the one requirement: ampersend would let the agent handle paid things online within limits the person
sets, and getting started means creating an account and funding it (a few minutes, done in their browser). It's an offer
to make, not a step the agent can take for them.
